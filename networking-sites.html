🌐Top professional networking sites

Each platform has a distinct job in your stack — pick 2–3 that match how you like to show up.

  1. LinkedIn

    Your public professional identity and recruiter magnet.

    Best for job seekers Best for hiring visibility Best for thought leadership
    Top 3 moves on this platform
    1. Optimize your headline for the exact keywords recruiters and peers search.
    2. DM thoughtfully after profile views—reference a specific post or shared context.
    3. Join three niche groups and post weekly with one lesson or question, not generic motivation.

    Sign in to LinkedIn

  2. Wellfound (formerly AngelList Talent)

    Where startup hiring intent is explicit—so conversations start closer to a yes.

    Best for founders Best for startup job seekers Best for early-stage hiring
    Top 3 moves on this platform
    1. Keep a tight narrative: stage, stack, traction—remove vague adjectives.
    2. Refresh signals weekly (projects, availability) so you appear active, not stale.
    3. Ask for warm intros for specific roles, not "anything in startups."

    Sign in to Wellfound

  3. Meetup

    Niche communities meeting in person; warm conversations at low friction.

    Best for niche communities Best for local in-person networking Best for skill-building peer groups
    Top 3 moves on this platform
    1. RSVP early and attend consistently; regularity makes you trustworthy.
    2. Follow up within 24 hours: "Appreciated your point about X; let's stay in touch."
    3. Volunteer to speak or lead a session; visible expertise builds fast credibility.

    Go to Meetup

  4. Slack (& Discord communities)

    Async depth: low-pressure questions, niche expertise, and slow-burn relationships.

    Best for niche communities Best for daily asynchronous chat Best for questions and advice
    Top 3 moves on this platform
    1. Join 2-3 communities; be active in one. Scattered presence reads as noise.
    2. Answer questions generously, ask specific ones. Build reputation for taste and knowledge.
    3. DM thoughtfully: reference something they wrote, offer help, then propose a call if momentum builds.

    Go to Slack

  5. X (formerly Twitter)

    Public signal and distribution; where your thoughts can reach the right people at scale.

    Best for public voice Best for thought leadership Best for reaching influencers
    Top 3 moves on this platform
    1. Post one original insight weekly: a lesson, observation, or opinion. Skip generic retweets.
    2. Reply thoughtfully to 3-5 accounts in your niche weekly; build relationship before asking.
    3. Use a consistent newsletter or link in bio; give people a reason to stay after the tweet.

    Sign in to X

  6. Substack (or blog)

    Your owned audience and deep ideas; a space to build trust and showcase thinking.

    Best for building owned audience Best for longer-form writing Best for email followup
    Top 3 moves on this platform
    1. Commit to two pieces monthly; consistency builds subscribers better than one epic post.
    2. Lead with insight, not self-promotion; subscribers come for your ideas, not your resume.
    3. Use insights from chats: "A founder recently asked me about X" makes content relatable.

    Go to Substack

  7. Upwork

    Paid work and proof; first freelance opportunities and reputation building.

    Best for freelancers Best for paid proof of work Best for side projects
    Top 3 moves on this platform
    1. Write proposals that diagnose: show you understand the problem before pitching the solution.
    2. Refresh your profile weekly: update availability, rates, and featured projects.
    3. Ask repeat clients to write reviews; 5-star feedback compounds over time and beats portfolio links.

    Go to Upwork

  8. Eventbrite

    Discover and host events; see who else is interested in the problem you care about.

    Best for finding events Best for hosting events Best for large gatherings
    Top 3 moves on this platform
    1. Filter by your niche topics and mark your calendar; show up in person and follow up.
    2. Create a 30-second opener if you host so you can give a personal welcome to attendees.
    3. Add a CRM or email signup to your events; attendee lists are gold for outreach.

    Go to Eventbrite

  9. Medium (or dev.to)

    Published proof and built-in reach; your writing can find the right audience easily.

    Best for writing distribution Best for long-form articles Best for reach in tech niches
    Top 3 moves on this platform
    1. Publish one article monthly; lean into what you know that others don't.
    2. Use clear headlines that match how people search (SEO helps visibility).
    3. Engage with comments; show up, answer questions, build a voice people recognize.

    Go to Medium

    🤝Event follow-up scripts

    Speed and specificity are the variables — the faster and more personal the follow-up, the stronger the connection holds.

    • Same night · light touch

      "Great meeting you at [event] — loved your point about [X]. I'd enjoy staying in touch on [topic]."

    • Next day · value add

      "Following up from [event] — here's [link/resource] related to what we discussed. If you're open, could we do a 15-min call next week to compare notes on [Y]?"

    • Intro offer

      "If it's useful, I can introduce you to [person/org] working on [Z]. Want me to draft something forwardable?"
